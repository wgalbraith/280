<p>How do we use mathematical shorthand to write very large and very small numbers? We focus on the most important digits, and use an abbreviation to avoid writing out the insignificant digits.</p>
<p>Usually the only significant information is in the first couple of digits and the location in place value that they occupy.</p>
<h2 id="scientific-notation">Scientific Notation</h2>
<ul>
<li>Allows us to compactly write very large or very small numbers</li>
<li>Creates a shorthand that allows us to simplify calculation and avoid errors</li>
<li>It is common practice to use a form of scientific notation when we <it>speak</it> about large numbers. For example, if a city's budget was $3,250,000,000, it is common to say "three point two five billion".

<h2 id="representing-large-numbers">Representing large numbers</h2>
<p>There are different ways to express scientific notation</p>
<ul>
<li>On paper <span class="math display">$1.23 \times 10^4$</span></li>
<li>On a calculator/computer <code>1.23E4</code></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="a-very-large-number">A very large number</h2>
<ul>
<li>Avogadro’s Number: <span class="math display">$6.02 \times 10^{23}$</span></li>
<li><span class="math display">$10^{1}=10$</span></li>
<li><span class="math display">$10^{2} = 10 \times 10$</span></li>
<li><span class="math display">$10^{3} = 10 \times 10 \times 10$</span></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="a-very-small-number">A very small number</h2>
<ul>
<li>Gravitational Constant: <span class="math display">$6.67 \times 10^{-11} (m^3 kg^{-1} s^{-2})$</span></li>
<li><span class="math display">$10^{-1} = \frac{1}{10}$</span></li>
<li><span class="math display">$10^{-2} = \frac{1}{10 \times 10}$</span></li>
<li><span class="math display">$10^{-3} = \frac{1}{10 \times 10 \times 10}$</span></li>
</ul>

<h2 id="multiplying-and-dividing-large-numbers">Multiplying and dividing large numbers</h2>
<p><span class="math display"> $$10^a \cdot 10^b = 10^{a+b}$$ </span></p>
<p><span class="math display"> $$c \cdot 10^a \cdot d \cdot 10^b = c \cdot d \cdot 10^{a+b}$$ </span></p>
<p><span class="math display"> $$\frac{10^a}{10^b} = 10^{a-b}$$ </span></p>
<p><span class="math display"> $$\frac{c \cdot 10^a}{d \cdot 10^b} = \frac{c}{d} \cdot 10^{a-b}$$ </span></p>
<p>You can combine these rules if you are multiplying and dividing several numbers in scientific notation.</p>
<h2 id="common-powers">Common Powers</h2>
<p>Notice that these prefixes correspond to english language quantities you are already familiar with. We often express large numbers verbally, so there isn’t much new to learn to use metric prefixes or scientific notation.</p>
<p>For example, 1 billion joules is the same as a gigajoule is the same as <span class="math display"> $1 \cdot 10^{9}$ </span> joules is the same as 1,000,000,000 joules.</p>
