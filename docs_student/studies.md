<h1>Observational Studies and Experiments</h1>

<h2>Observational Studies</h2>
<p>
An <strong>observational study</strong> is a study in which researchers observe individuals
and measure variables of interest without attempting to influence the responses.
The goal is to describe patterns or associations that naturally occur.
</p>

<p>
In an observational study, researchers do not assign treatments or impose conditions.
Instead, they collect data as it already exists. Because of this, observational studies
can identify relationships between variables, but they generally cannot establish
cause-and-effect.
</p>

<hr>

<h2>Experiments</h2>
<p>
An <strong>experiment</strong> is a study in which researchers deliberately impose
a treatment on individuals in order to observe a response. The goal is to determine
whether the treatment causes a change in the response variable.
</p>

<p>
In an experiment, researchers actively control the explanatory variable by assigning
subjects to different groups, often including a treatment group and a control group.
Well-designed experiments allow for stronger conclusions about cause-and-effect.
</p>

<hr>

<h2>Key Differences</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Observational study:</strong> observes without intervention</li>
<li><strong>Experiment:</strong> imposes treatments and measures outcomes</li>
<li><strong>Causation:</strong> can typically only be established in experiments</li>
<li><strong>Association:</strong> can be identified in both, but especially observational studies</li>
</ul>

<hr>

<h2>Example</h2>
<p>
Suppose researchers want to study the relationship between exercise and blood pressure.
</p>

<ul>
<li>
<strong>Observational study:</strong> Researchers record how much people exercise and
measure their blood pressure without influencing their behavior.
</li>
<li>
<strong>Experiment:</strong> Researchers assign participants to an exercise program
(treatment group) or no program (control group), then compare blood pressure outcomes.
</li>
</ul>

<p>
In the observational study, researchers may find an association between exercise and
lower blood pressure, but they cannot conclude that exercise causes the change.
In the experiment, because the treatment is controlled, researchers can make stronger
causal conclusions.
</p>
