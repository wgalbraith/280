<h1 id="exponential">Exponential</h1>
<p>The word exponential makes this concept sound unnecessarily
difficult.</p>
<p>If a function is exponential, the y-values of any points on the line
that are the same distance apart on the x-axis will have the same ratio.
This is similar to linear functions where the y-values of any points on
the line that are the same distance apart on the x-axis have the same
difference when subtracted.</p>
<p>Exponential models arise when the change in a quantity is
proportional to the amount of the quantity. That is, the slope of an
exponential function at any point is equal to the value of the function
at any point multiplied by a number.</p>
<h2 id="doubling-time">Doubling Time</h2>
<p>Exponential growth means that if you measure how long it takes a
quantity to double, the time to double from any current amount will be
the same.</p>
<p>There is nothing special about the doubling time, it is just a
convenient ratio of 2:1. Keep in mind that the ratio between the two
points is the same for any two equal time intervals.</p>

<h2 id="doubling-time-1">Doubling Time</h2>
<p>A property of an exponential function, is that in a given interval of
time, the value increases by a fixed ratio.</p>
<p>So, if the function doubles over a time, let’s call it <span
class="math inline">t</span>, if we wait for a time <span
class="math inline">t</span> at any time, the value of the function
doubles.</p>
<h2 id="slope-proportional-to-function-value">Slope proportional to
function value</h2>
<p>The exponential is defined as a function whose slope is proportional
to its value.</p>
<p>The function that is exactly equal to its slope is <span
class="math inline">y = e^x</span>.</p>
<h2 id="exponential-notation">Exponential notation</h2>
<p>You’ve probably seen notation like <span
class="math inline">x^2</span> or <span class="math inline">x^3</span>
several times before. In exponential notation, our symbol for a
variable, <span class="math inline">x</span> is in the exponent.</p>
<p>For example <span class="math inline">2^x</span> or <span
class="math inline">3^x</span>.</p>
<p>Recall that <span class="math inline">1 = 2^0</span>, <span
class="math inline">2 = 2^1</span>, <span class="math inline">2 \cdot 2
= 2^2 = 4</span>, etc. So, <span class="math inline">2^x</span> means to
multiply 2 by itself x times.</p>

<h1 id="exponential-computations">Exponential Computations</h1>
<h1 id="forward">Forward</h1>
<p>Often an exponential is used to represent a population or an amount
of something.</p>
<p>If we want to find the amount or population at a certain time or
other independent variable, we substitute our variables and compute the
amount.</p>
<p><span class="math display">population = P_0 e^{at}</span></p>
<ul>
<li><span class="math inline">P_0</span> is the population when <span
class="math inline">t=0</span>.</li>
<li><span class="math inline">a</span> tells us how fast the population
is growing.</li>
<li><span class="math inline">t</span> is the time.</li>
<li>We have to be certain that the units for <span
class="math inline">a</span> and <span class="math inline">t</span>
match.</li>
</ul>
<h1 id="inverse-or-logarithm">Inverse or Logarithm</h1>
<p>If we want to find the time at which a population reaches a certain
number (dependent variable), we must use the inverse of the exponential.
This inverse is the logarithm.</p>
<h1 id="brute-force-inverse">Brute force inverse</h1>
<p>To find the inverse, you can also guess and adjust your number for
the independent variable until you match the dependent variable.</p>

<h1 id="inverses">Inverses</h1>
<p>Note that the logarithm and exponential are inverses of each
other.</p>
<p><span class="math display">\log e^x = x</span></p>
<p><span class="math display">e^{\log x} = x</span></p>
<p>Where in this case, log means the natural log.</p>
<h1 id="inverting-a-logarithm">Inverting a Logarithm</h1>
<p>You may have the equation below and want to find t.</p>
<p><span class="math display">y=A e^{b\cdot t}</span></p>
<p>Your strategy is to manipulate the equation so that you can use the
inverse.</p>
<p><span class="math display">\frac{y}{A} = e^{b \cdot t}</span></p>
<p>At this point we can take the logarithm of both sides since the
logarithm is the inverse of the exponential function.</p>
<p><span class="math display"> \log(y/A) = log(e^{b \cdot t}) = b \cdot
t</span></p>
<p><span class="math display"> t = \frac{\log(y/A)}{b}</span></p>
<h1 id="computation">Computation</h1>
<p>Note that the natural log is used to invert <span
class="math inline">e^x</span>, while the base 10 log is used to invert
<span class="math inline">10^x</span>.</p>
<p>On calculators and computers, the natural log is referred to as
<code>ln</code> or <code>log</code> while the base 10 log is referred to
as <code>log</code> or <code>log10</code>.</p>
<p>Be sure you have tested your functions and know which one to use.</p>
